üìã Common Inclusion Types

Internal (Inclusions)

  • Crystal: Mineral crystal trapped inside
  • Feather: Small crack/fracture
  • Cloud: Cluster of tiny pinpoints
  • Pinpoint: Tiny crystal (white/black)

  • Twinning Wisp: Growth irregularity
  • Internal Graining: Growth lines
  • Knot: Crystal reaching surface
  • Cavity: Opening at surface

  • Scratch: Linear mark on surface
  • Nick: Small chip at facet junction
  • Pit: Small opening on surface
  • Chip: Broken piece from surface
  • Abrasion: Worn/scratched facet edges
  • Natural: Original rough surface
  • Extra Facet: Additional facet
  • Polish Lines: Polishing marks
  • Burn Mark: Hazy from excess heat
  • Rough Girdle: Unpolished girdle

üìú Certification Labs

GIA - Gemological Institute of America

Industry standard. Most consistent and respected. Strictest grading. Created the 4Cs system.

Best for: High-value purchases, colorless diamonds, resale value

AGS - American Gem Society

Excellent reputation. Known for cut grading‚Äî0-10 scale with "Ideal" designation. Comparable to GIA.

Best for: Cut quality focus, "hearts and arrows" diamonds

IGI - International Gemological Institute

Reputable, widely used. Sometimes grades 1-2 grades softer than GIA. Very common for lab-grown.

Best for: Lab-grown diamonds, budget purchases

EGL - European Gemological Lab

Significantly softer grading (1-3+ grades). EGL USA closed. International locations vary wildly.

Caution: Treat as 1-3 grades lower. Price accordingly.

GSI, GCAL, Others

Lesser known labs. Grading consistency varies. Often used by chain stores and online discounters.

Caution: May be 1-2 grades soft. Compare to GIA carefully.

When comparing non-GIA to GIA, adjust:

  • IGI: -0.5 to 1 grade
  • EGL: -1 to 3 grades
  • GSI: -1 to 2 grades

üî¨ Natural vs Lab-Grown Diamonds

Natural Diamonds

  • Formed over 1-3 billion years
  • 100+ miles underground
  • Finite supply
  • Holds resale value better
  • Traditional/sentimental value
  • Contains natural inclusions
  • May have nitrogen (Type Ia)

Lab-Grown Diamonds

  • Created in weeks
  • HPHT or CVD process
  • Unlimited supply
  • 60-80% less expensive
  • Chemically identical
  • Often higher clarity/color
  • Type IIa (very pure)

Identification Methods

  • Inscription: Often laser-inscribed "LG" or lab name
  • UV fluorescence: Different patterns
  • Phosphorescence: HPHT glows after UV off
  • Growth patterns: Different from natural
  • Metallic inclusions: HPHT may have
  • Testing devices: DiamondView, etc.
Always disclose lab-grown. Selling as natural is fraud.

Core Setting Principles

üíé Setting Types ‚Äî Step-by-Step

Prong Setting

Metal claws grip stone at girdle. Maximum light exposure. 4-prong classic, 6-prong more secure for larger stones.

Required Burs
Setting bur (size of stone or slightly smaller), Hart bur, Ball bur, Cup bur
Step-by-Step
  1. Inspect mounting for defects, check prong alignment
  2. Pre-polish inside of head and surrounding area
  3. Mark seat height with dividers (1/3 down from prong top)
  4. Cut seats one prong at a time‚Äî30-50% depth of prong thickness
  5. Check stone sits level; adjust seats as needed
  6. Remove burrs with knife-edge graver
  7. Pre-bend prong tips slightly inward
  8. Place stone, tighten in cross pattern (N-S-E-W)
  9. Shape prong tips with barrette file
  10. Finish tips with cup bur matching prong diameter
  11. Clean up with graver, polish with rubber wheel
Key Technique
VECTOR APPROACH: Push prongs toward center of stone, not just down. This creates better grip. Seat depth = 30-50% of prong thickness. Cut one prong at a time to avoid over-cutting. Stone should contact prong 1/3 up from girdle.
Common Errors
Under-cut seats (stone sits high), uneven seat heights, bent prongs (push don't bend), cup bur too small (creates groove)

Bezel Setting

Metal rim completely surrounds girdle. Most secure setting. Protects stone edges. Ideal for active lifestyles and softer stones.

Required Burs
Setting bur, Ball bur, Cylinder bur, Hart bur for bearing
Step-by-Step
  1. Check bezel height‚Äîshould cover 0.5-1mm above girdle
  2. Cut bearing (ledge) inside bezel at proper height
  3. Bearing angle should match stone pavilion angle
  4. Pre-polish inside of bezel
  5. Seat stone, verify it sits level and centered
  6. Push bezel at 12 o'clock position first
  7. Move to 6 o'clock (opposite side)
  8. Then 3 and 9 o'clock
  9. Work around, pushing in small sections
  10. Burnish to smooth and tighten
  11. Clean up top edge, add milgrain if desired
Key Technique
OPPOSITE SIDES METHOD: Always push opposite sides to keep stone centered. Fine silver bezels are softest/easiest. For thick bezels, use rocker pusher. Start at top of bezel, rock downward. Never push directly on stone.
Common Errors
Bezel too tall (obscures stone), uneven pressure (stone shifts), pushing too hard (stone cracks), bearing too deep (stone sits low)

Channel Setting

Stones held between two parallel metal walls. Clean modern lines. Very secure when done properly.

Required Burs
Setting bur, Hart bur, Flat graver, 90¬∞ graver
Step-by-Step
  1. Verify all stones are exactly same size
  2. Mark seat positions on both channel walls
  3. Cut individual seats (not grooves) for each stone
  4. Seats should be at identical heights on both sides
  5. All seats same depth‚Äîstone tops must align
  6. Place stones, check alignment before setting
  7. Use channel pusher or flat punch to push walls
  8. Work from center outward
  9. Hammer set for hard metals, burnish for soft
  10. File channel edges straight and even
  11. Bright-cut edges for clean finish
Key Technique
PRECISION IS EVERYTHING: Cut individual seats, not continuous grooves (stones will rock). All stones must be identical size. Test fit before final setting. For hard metals like platinum, use reciprocating hammer.
Common Errors
Grooves instead of seats (stones rock), unmatched stone sizes, walls not parallel, uneven stone heights

Pav√©/Bead Setting

Small stones set close together (~0.1mm apart) held by tiny metal beads raised with gravers. Creates diamond-encrusted look.

Required Burs
Ball bur, Setting bur, Hart bur, 90¬∞ graver, Beading tool
Step-by-Step
  1. Layout and mark stone positions (template helps)
  2. Drill pilot holes at each position
  3. Open holes with ball bur sized to stone
  4. Cut bearing with setting bur or hart bur
  5. Cut all seats BEFORE setting any stones
  6. Set stones starting from center, work outward
  7. Raise beads using onglette or round graver
  8. Push 4 beads per stone (or 3 for shared-bead)
  9. Shape beads with beading tool
  10. Bright-cut background for sparkle
  11. Clean and polish carefully
Key Technique
GRAVER CONTROL IS KEY: Practice raising beads on brass first. Cut toward stone, push metal up into bead. 4 beads minimum per stone. Shared beads (between stones) save time but require precision. Stone spacing 0.1-0.15mm.
Common Errors
Beads too small (won't hold), uneven bead sizes, cutting into stone girdle, stones too far apart (looks sparse)

Flush/Gypsy Setting

Stone sits level with or slightly below metal surface. Sleek modern look. Very secure. Ideal for men's rings and active wear.

Required Burs
Drill bit, Ball bur (stone diameter), Setting bur, Hart bur, Burnisher
Step-by-Step
  1. Mark center point precisely
  2. Drill pilot hole (smaller than stone)
  3. Open hole with ball bur = stone diameter
  4. Create funnel opening wider than hole
  5. Cut bearing with setting/hart bur at correct depth
  6. Stone girdle should sit just below surface
  7. Drop stone into seat
  8. Burnish metal over girdle evenly all around
  9. Work in opposite-sides pattern
  10. Push metal, don't scrape
  11. Final burnish for smooth surface
Key Technique
FUNNEL OPENING: Make funnel wider than you think‚Äîallows burnisher to push metal over. Metal thickness must be greater than stone depth. Test seat depth before burnishing. For colored stones, cut bearing slightly wider.
Common Errors
Funnel too narrow (can't burnish), hole off-center, bearing too deep (stone falls through), metal too thin

V-Prong Setting

Protects pointed ends of princess, marquise, pear shapes. Metal forms V-shape cradling the point.

Required Burs
Hart bur, Bud bur, Flat graver, Knife-edge graver
Step-by-Step
  1. Mark seat height on inside of V-prong
  2. Cut horizontal line at seat height with hart bur
  3. Cut vertical line down center of V
  4. Create void for stone point using bud bur
  5. Ensure cavity exists BELOW and ABOVE point
  6. Stone point must never touch metal
  7. Smooth seat with flat graver
  8. Place stone, verify point is protected
  9. Push V-prong over stone crown
  10. File excess metal forming at V apex
  11. Burnish smooth
Key Technique
PROTECT THE POINT: Always create void around point‚Äîpressure on unprotected point = chip or break. Stone point should "float" in V cavity. Cut no deeper than 1/3 into prong thickness.
Common Errors
Point touches metal (will chip), cavity too shallow, V-prong pushed unevenly, point not centered in V

Tension Setting

Stone held only by pressure of metal. Stone appears to float. Very dramatic but technically demanding.

Required Burs
Setting bur, Cylinder bur, Flat graver
Step-by-Step
  1. ONLY use purpose-made tension mountings
  2. Verify stone hardness 8+ (diamond, sapphire, ruby)
  3. Mounting must be properly heat-treated
  4. Cut precise seats in both sides of gap
  5. Seats must be exactly parallel
  6. Open gap slightly with spreader
  7. Place stone in seats
  8. Release spreader‚Äîmetal should grip firmly
  9. Stone should not rock or rotate
  10. Final adjustment with gentle pressure
  11. Test security thoroughly
Key Technique
NOT FOR SOFT STONES: Only diamonds, rubies, sapphires. Mounting metallurgy is critical‚Äîproper spring temper required. Do not attempt on DIY mountings. Stone must be calibrated to gap width.
Common Errors
Soft stones (will crack), improper mounting metal, uneven seat heights, gap too wide/narrow

Burnish/Hammer Setting

Variation of flush setting. Metal is hammered or burnished over stone. Creates texture around stone.

Required Burs
Ball bur, Setting bur, Planishing hammer, Burnisher
Step-by-Step
  1. Drill and open seat as with flush setting
  2. Cut bearing for stone
  3. Leave more metal around opening than flush setting
  4. Seat stone level
  5. Use small planishing hammer around stone
  6. Tap metal down over girdle in small increments
  7. Work around stone evenly
  8. Alternate with burnisher for control
  9. Create desired texture or smooth finish
  10. Final burnish to secure
Key Technique
TEXTURE CONTROL: Hammer creates texture, burnisher smooths. Combine for desired effect. More risky than standard flush‚Äîpractice on brass with CZs first.
Common Errors
Hammer too hard (cracks stone), uneven hammering, insufficient metal to grip

üîß Chain Repair Guide

Soldering Tips

  • Use easy solder for most chain repairs
  • Heat entire link, not just joint
  • Apply solder from inside if possible
  • Flatten curb/figaro links after soldering
  • Check adjacent links for heat damage
  • Pickle and clean before returning
  • Use third hand or tweezers to hold

Laser Welding Tips

  • Preferred for: rope, wheat, byzantine, mesh
  • Lower power for thin links
  • Multiple small welds stronger than one big
  • May need filler wire for gaps
  • Won't work on plated chains
  • Clean thoroughly first

When to Decline Repair

  • Kinked snake or herringbone
  • Severely worn links (thin spots)
  • Multiple breaks close together
  • Hollow chain with crushed links
  • Plated chain (solder won't match)
  • Cost exceeds replacement value

Link Replacement

  • Match link size exactly
  • Keep spare links from sizing
  • Figaro: maintain pattern
  • Order matching links from supplier
  • Check color match (rhodium, etc.)
  • Two solder joints per new link

üîß Techniques

Compound Application

Apply compound while wheel is spinning. Light touch‚Äîcompound should transfer, not glob. Reapply frequently in small amounts.

Work Direction

Hold work BELOW center of wheel. Wheel rotation should push DOWN into your hands, not grab and throw.

Pressure

Light pressure for cutting, lighter for polishing. Let the compound do the work. Heavy pressure = heat = problems.

Movement

Keep work moving constantly. Staying in one spot creates grooves, uneven polish, or heat damage.

Edge Safety

Present edges TRAILING (pointing away from wheel rotation). Leading edges catch and throw work.

Ring Polishing

Use finger or mandrel inside ring. Polish outside first, then inside with goblet/felt buff.

Chain Polishing

Wrap around dowel or hold in leather. Never let chain contact bare wheel‚Äîwill snag.

Progression Rule

Never skip grits. Tripoli ‚Üí White Diamond ‚Üí Rouge. Each step only removes previous step's scratches.

Cross-Contamination

ONE compound per wheel‚ÄîNEVER mix. Label wheels clearly. Store separately.

Heat Check

If too hot to hold, stop. Heat damages solder joints, loosens stones, warps thin metal.

Bench Jeweler's Tips

Hard-won wisdom for soldering, setting, repairs, and more

üî• Soldering

  • Flux everything‚Äîprevents firescale and helps solder flow
  • Heat the metal, not the solder‚Äîsolder flows toward heat
  • Clean joints are essential‚Äîsolder won't flow on dirty/oxidized metal
  • Pieces must touch‚Äîsolder fills gaps up to 0.1mm max
  • Equal mass = heat both pieces equally; unequal = heat larger piece more
  • Work from hard to easy solder on multi-joint pieces
  • Pickle removes flux/oxides‚ÄîNEVER put steel in pickle (contaminates)
  • Quench in water before pickle to avoid splashing hot acid
  • Use copper tongs only in pickle‚Äîsteel turns silver pink
  • Paste flux for easy solder, liquid flux for hard solder
  • Pre-place solder chips before heating for precision work
  • Solder pallions: 1mm squares for small joints, 2mm for larger
  • Reduce flame and move faster as solder approaches flow temp
  • If solder balls up: too much flux, dirty metal, or not hot enough
  • Binding wire: use iron binding wire, remove before pickle

üíé Stone Setting

  • Examine stone under magnification before setting‚Äînote any damage
  • Check fit BEFORE cutting seat‚Äîeasier to remove metal than add
  • Push prongs toward center, never just bend them down
  • Work opposite sides: N-S then E-W keeps stone centered
  • Seat depth: 30-50% of prong thickness
  • Stone should sit 1/3 up from prong base
  • Pre-polish inside setting before placing stone
  • Fragile stones order: Last to set, first to remove
  • Heat-sensitive: opal, pearl, emerald, tanzanite‚Äîremove before torch
  • Never push on table of stone‚Äîpush on crown facets
  • Use brass pusher on soft stones to avoid scratches
  • Cup bur must match prong diameter exactly
  • Bezel: push opposite sides, work around in small sections
  • Channel: all stones must be exactly same size
  • Pav√©: cut ALL seats before setting ANY stones
  • Check prongs with loupe after setting‚Äîshould grip at girdle

üìè Ring Sizing

  • Measure finger at end of day (fingers swell)
  • Consider knuckle size‚Äîring must pass over
  • Wide bands (>6mm) size up 0.5-1 size
  • Comfort fit adds ~0.25-0.5 perceived size
  • Stretch max: 2 sizes up (1 size for thin shanks)
  • Size down: cut out section, never just squeeze closed
  • Match solder color to metal‚Äîespecially white gold
  • Refinish sizing area to blend with original finish
  • Check for stones before heating‚Äîremove heat-sensitive
  • Spring rings/tension settings: don't size without adjusting spring
  • Eternity bands: add/remove stones, can't traditionally size
  • Tungsten, titanium, ceramic: cannot be sized‚Äîmust replace

üîß Repairs

  • Quote worst-case scenario, pleasant surprise if less

  • Worn shanks: add shank reinforcement or replace bottom half
  • Chain repair: match link size and style exactly
  • Spring ring replacement: match diameter, use quality findings
  • Clasp conversion: ensure weight balance with new clasp
  • Hinge repair: replace both knuckles if worn
  • Pearl restringing: knot between each pearl, use silk or Softflex
  • Watch band pins: measure carefully, use spring bar tool
  • Clean before and after every repair
  • Test clasps and catches before returning to customer

Torch Reference

Flame types, gas settings, and annealing colors

üî• Flame Types

Reducing Flame

Excess fuel, less oxygen. Bushy, yellow-orange inner cone.

  • Deposits carbon on metal
  • Use for: Annealing, initial heating
  • Prevents oxidation
  • Cooler than neutral

Neutral Flame

Balanced fuel/oxygen. Sharp, well-defined inner cone.

  • Ideal for most soldering
  • Clean, no oxidation or carbon
  • Maximum heat efficiency
  • Standard working flame

Oxidizing Flame

Excess oxygen. Short, pointed, hissing inner cone.

  • Causes oxidation/firescale
  • Hottest flame type
  • Rarely used intentionally
  • Can make solder not flow

‚öóÔ∏è Rhodium Plating Process

Standard Procedure

  1. Clean thoroughly ‚Äî Ultrasonic, steam, ensure grease-free
  2. Electro-clean ‚Äî Alkaline cleaner, reverse polarity, 30 sec

  4. Acid dip ‚Äî 10% sulfuric acid, 10-15 sec (activates surface)
  5. Rinse ‚Äî Distilled water

‚ö†Ô∏è Troubleshooting

Common Problems

  • Peeling/flaking: Poor cleaning, contamination
  • Dark/gray color: Voltage too high, time too long
  • Uneven coverage: Inadequate cleaning, poor contact
  • Burns (dark spots): Too high voltage, too close

Patina & Oxidation

Antiquing techniques and liver of sulfur formulas

üß™ Liver of Sulfur (LOS)

Basic Formula

Hot water (not boiling) + pea-sized chunk LOS = light patina solution

Stronger solution = faster, darker results. Temperature matters‚Äîwarmer = faster.

Process

  1. Clean piece thoroughly (brass brush, degrease)
  2. Dip in LOS solution‚Äîwatch color develop
  3. Rinse immediately in cold water to stop
  4. Repeat dips for darker color
  5. Relieve high points with polishing cloth or pumice
  6. Seal with wax if desired

Mash yolk, seal with silver in bag. Sulfur fumes create patina overnight. Gentle, natural process.
